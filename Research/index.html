Research

My research explores the intricate processes involved in atmospheric chemistry and physics, particularly focusing on aerosol formation and oxidation processes under varying atmospheric conditions, and its environmental and climate effects. Using a combination of laboratory experiments, environmental modeling, and field measurements, I delve into the dynamics of chemical and physical transformations within aerosols. This comprehensive approach not only enhances our understanding of aerosol behavior but also contributes to the broader field of atmospheric sciences by linking microscopic processes to macroscopic atmospheric phenomena. My goal is to provide insights that can inform both air quality management and climate modeling, ensuring that our strategies for addressing environmental and atmospheric challenges are grounded in robust scientific knowledge.

1. Secondary Organic Aerosol (SOA) Formation

I study the oxidation of volatile organic compounds (VOC) that lead to formation of secondary organic aerosol (SOA). The VOCs of interest include biogenic VOCs (e.g., monoterpenes) and anthropogenic hydrocarbons (e.g., aromatic VOCs). We perform controlled laboratory experiments of VOC oxidation and compositional measurements of the gas- and particle-phase products using advanced analytical instruments. We combine measurements and kinetic simulations to develop mechanistic understanding of VOC oxidation and SOA formation.

2. Oxidation Mechanism Development

I propose condensed oxidation mechanism for volatile organic compounds (VOC) like isoprene and alpha-Pinene based on existing explicit mechanism and recent published research findings. We perform kinetic modeling of VOC oxidation and compared the modeling results to controlled laboratory experiments to refine kinetic parameters that are important for the fate and pathway of key species. The developed condensed mechanism is much smaller and can be widely applied in regional models.
